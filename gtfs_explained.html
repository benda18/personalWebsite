GTFS Explained

The General Transit Feed Specification is the de-factor standard format for sharing public transportation schedudling information. GTFS feeds are critical for the reliable and accurate implementation of services like Google Transit, the service for which the specification was created for initially in 2006. However, the format has evolved to become the backgone of sharing schedling info not only with app developers but also for the sharing of scheduling data between technology systems within transit authorities themselves (i.e. between CAD-AVL systems and digital headsigns.

At a Basic Level GTFS Shows Where and When Vehicles Are Located

Transit vehicle location both sptially and temporally are the basis of the feed. stop_times.txt holds this base data.





Transit Stops are the Foundation of the Feed

Transit stops are the foundation of the feed. They are where passengers interface with the transportation network. Stop info is stored and accessed from stops.txt. Unlike the network for private automobiles which can be accessed anywhere, anytime by anyone, transit users are burdened by the need to arrive at a pre-definied stop at a specific time to catch a ride. In the GTFS feed, everything revolves around transit stop location.

Stop-Level Arrival Times

Knowing the transit stop locations alone isn't enough info for most transit riders because most services run on an infrequent and/or a dynamic schedule based on the time-of-day and day-of-week. Therefore, the feed needs to convey when every transit vehicle will arrive at any particular stop location. This information is found in stop_times.txt. Stop_times.txt is the largest file in most feeds because it represents the geo-temporal location of every vehcile at every point in time throughout an entire transit week. When you join stop_times.txt with stops.txt you can show how vehicles travel throughout a transit day, but you're still missing lots of information: what routes visit which stops, how Monday service differs from Sunday service, transfer locations, and much more.

Route information

The next level of information that gets integrated into the feed is information about individual routes. Routes.txt assigns a specific route name with each trip.





Calendar Information

So far, we've deconstructed the feed based on time-of-day. The next step is to break out feed data based on the day of week because service patterns vary typically on weekdays, weekends and holidays. Data is stored in calendar.txt
